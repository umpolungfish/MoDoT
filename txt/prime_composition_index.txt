\begin{abstract}
We show that multiplication of positive integers is a composition operation in a
twelve-opcode fork-fuse grammar, and that primality is derivable directly from
how the composite numbers compose. Each integer $n$ is presented as a directed
cycle $\Rc_n$ of period $n$. We define a product $\Rc_a \cprod \Rc_b = \Rc_{ab}$,
realized concretely by tiling $\Rc_{ab}$ into $b$ consecutive fork-fuse bubbles
each of arm length $a$; the realization closes, in the sense that
$\fuse\circ\fork$ restores identity on a transformed object, and its
reconvergence multiplicity is exactly $\beta=b$. The composites are then, by
definition and without residue, the image of this product on nontrivial factors:
$n\geq 2$ is composite if and only if $\Rc_n = \Rc_a \cprod \Rc_b$ for some
$a,b\geq 2$. The primes are the complement. Concretely, if one forms every
product of two nontrivial cycles up to bound $N$ and removes the periods that
occur, exactly the primes up to $N$ remain: this is the sieve of Eratosthenes,
stated as the removal of the product image, and the operation removed is a
genuine composition in the grammar rather than an arithmetic test applied from
outside. The $\cprod$-irreducible cycles are exactly the prime cycles, and the
unique factorization of the integers transfers verbatim to $(\{\Rc_n\},\cprod)$.
Every product, tiling, and closure asserted below is verified in the grammar's
reference implementation, and the family is exhibited for all primes up to $103$.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} primality \(\cdot\) sieve of Eratosthenes \(\cdot\)
cycle graph \(\cdot\) fork-fuse product \(\cdot\) Frobenius \(\cdot\) fundamental
theorem of arithmetic \(\cdot\) machine-checked

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The usual way to certify that a number is prime is to test it: try the divisors
and report that none divide. That is a statement about the number in isolation.
This note takes the opposite direction. We build a product operation on a family
of directed graphs, one graph per integer, under which the composite numbers are
literally the products of smaller numbers, and we then obtain the primes as the
residue: the numbers that no product reaches. Primality is derived from the
composites, not tested against the candidate.

The setting is a directed-graph grammar of twelve opcodes with a distinguished
fork $\fork$ (the only opcode that may branch, out-arity two) and fuse $\fuse$
(the only opcode that may merge, in-arity two), in the tradition of Frobenius
structures in traced monoidal categories \cite{CarboniWalters1987,Kelly1980}. A
program is a directed graph; it \emph{closes} when a $\fork$ splits, the arms do
genuine work, and a $\fuse$ restores identity, so that $\fuse\circ\fork$ acts as
the identity on a transformed object \cite{OddRingIndex}. The opcodes and the
composition verbs are collected for reference in \Cref{app:glossary}.

The integer $n$ is presented as the period-$n$ cycle $\Rc_n$. The whole content
of the note is one construction and its consequence. The construction
(\Cref{sec:product}) is a product $\Rc_a\cprod\Rc_b=\Rc_{ab}$ that multiplies the
periods and is realized by an explicit fork-fuse tiling whose reconvergence count
is one of the two factors. The consequence (\Cref{sec:sieve}) is that the
composites are the image of this product and the primes are its complement, which
is the sieve of Eratosthenes read inside the grammar. A companion note
\cite{OddRingIndex} reads a different integer, the parity signature, off the same
cycles; we record the parallel but do not lean on it.

\section{Integers as closed protocols}
\label{sec:protocols}
%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\begin{definition}[Period cycle]
\label{def:cycle}
For $n\geq 1$ the \emph{period-$n$ cycle} $\Rc_n$ is the directed cycle on $n$
nodes, each of in-degree and out-degree one. Its node count is $V(\Rc_n)=n$ and
its circuit rank is $\beta(\Rc_n)=E-V+C=1$.
\end{definition}

The node count is the magnitude, and it is injective in $n$. This already
distinguishes integers that a fixed-width labelling need not: in the reference
catalog the flat twelve-symbol imscription of $11$, $47$, and $83$ is one and the
same string, a projection to a finite-alphabet word that cannot separate an
unbounded family, whereas $\Rc_{11},\Rc_{47},\Rc_{83}$ are distinct objects. The
period carries the magnitude the tuple drops; the product below carries the
arithmetic.

\begin{definition}[Balanced closing protocol]
\label{def:balanced}
A \emph{closing protocol} is a directed graph with one source, one sink, and a
set of fork-fuse pairs $(\fork_i,\fuse_i)$ in which the two in-arms of each
$\fuse_i$ trace back, along directed edges, to a common $\fork_i$ (ancestry
pairing) and at least one arm carries a work opcode, so that
$\fuse_i\circ\fork_i$ is the identity on a transformed object. Its
\emph{reconvergence multiplicity} $\beta$ is the number of such pairs. A closing
protocol is \emph{balanced with arm length $d$} if it is obtained by cutting a
period cycle into consecutive segments of equal length $d$ and closing each
segment by one fork-fuse bubble.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Circuit rank is not the closure]
\label{rem:barecycle}
$\Rc_n$ has circuit rank $1$ but reconvergence multiplicity $0$: it contains no
$\fork$ and no $\fuse$, so nothing splits and nothing fuses. Reconvergence
multiplicity counts fork-fuse dyads that actually close, and it is $\beta$, not
the circuit rank, that will carry the arithmetic. A bare cycle is not a closure.
\end{remark}

%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{The cycle product}
\label{sec:product}
%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

We now put multiplication into the grammar. The product of two cycles is again a
cycle, of the product period, and it comes with an explicit closed realization.

\begin{definition}[Cycle product]
\label{def:product}
For $a,b\geq 1$ the \emph{cycle product} is $\Rc_a \cprod \Rc_b := \Rc_{ab}$. Its
\emph{$b$-fold realization} is the balanced closing protocol that cuts $\Rc_{ab}$
into $b$ consecutive segments of length $a$ and closes each by one fork-fuse
bubble, the parenthesized block repeated $b$ times:
\[
\vdash\ \bigl(\,\fork\;[\,a\ \text{work opcodes}\,]\;\fuse\,\bigr)^{\,b}\ \dashv ,
\]
where the exponent $b$ denotes $b$ consecutive copies of the bubble.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[The product realizes multiplication]
\label{thm:product}
For all $a,b\geq 1$ the $b$-fold realization of $\Rc_a\cprod\Rc_b$ is a closing
protocol on $\Rc_{ab}$, and its reconvergence multiplicity is $\beta=b$.
Symmetrically the $a$-fold realization on the same object $\Rc_{ab}$ has
$\beta=a$. On objects the product is commutative and associative,
\[
\Rc_a\cprod\Rc_b=\Rc_{ab}=\Rc_b\cprod\Rc_a,\qquad
(\Rc_a\cprod\Rc_b)\cprod\Rc_c=\Rc_{abc}=\Rc_a\cprod(\Rc_b\cprod\Rc_c),
\]
with unit $\Rc_1$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The $b$ segments of length $a$ partition the $ab$ nodes of $\Rc_{ab}$ exactly,
since $a\cdot b=ab$; each segment closes by one bubble whose single fork pairs by
ancestry with its single fuse and whose arm carries work, so the realization is a
closing protocol with one pair per segment, $\beta=b$. Reading the same $ab$
nodes as $a$ segments of length $b$ gives the $a$-fold realization with
$\beta=a$. Commutativity and associativity on objects are the identities
$ab=ba$ and $(ab)c=a(bc)$ on the periods, and $\Rc_1$ is a unit because
$1\cdot n=n$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[The two realizations record which factor is the bubble count]
\label{rem:asymmetry}
The product is commutative on the underlying cycle, but a realization is not a
bare object: it also records, in $\beta$, how many bubbles the tiling used. The
$b$-fold and $a$-fold realizations of $\Rc_{ab}$ are the same object closed two
ways, with $\beta=b$ and $\beta=a$ respectively. This is why a single composite
period exhibits several values of $\beta$, one per way of writing it as a product
(\Cref{sec:sieve}).
\end{remark}

The tiling underlying \Cref{thm:product} exists for arm length $d$ precisely when
$d$ divides the period, which is the one fact the sieve will use:

\begin{lemma}[Admissible arm lengths are divisors]
\label{lem:divisor}
A balanced closing protocol of arm length $d$ on $\Rc_n$ exists if and only if
$d\mid n$, and it then has $\beta=n/d$ and is unique up to rotation.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Equal segments of length $d$ partition a cyclic sequence of $n$ nodes iff
$d\mid n$; when $d\nmid n$ the final segment is short, its bubble has an arm of
the wrong length, and the ancestry pairing fails at the last fuse, which is left
with an in-arm that traces to no fork. When $d\mid n$ the number of segments,
hence of pairs, is $n/d$, and rotating the cut by one node carries one partition
to another and exhausts them.
\end{proof}

%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{The composites are the image; the primes are the sieve residue}
\label{sec:sieve}
%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Everything now follows from \Cref{thm:product,lem:divisor}. Write
$\mathbb{N}_{\geq 2}$ for the integers at least $2$.

\begin{theorem}[Composites are exactly the product image]
\label{thm:composite}
For $n\geq 2$, the following are equivalent:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(i)] $n$ is composite;
\item[(ii)] $\Rc_n=\Rc_a\cprod\Rc_b$ for some $a,b\in\mathbb{N}_{\geq 2}$;
\item[(iii)] $\Rc_n$ carries a balanced closing protocol with reconvergence
             multiplicity $\beta$ strictly between $1$ and $n$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
(i)$\Leftrightarrow$(ii): $n$ is composite iff $n=ab$ with $a,b\geq 2$, which is
exactly $\Rc_n=\Rc_a\cprod\Rc_b$ by \Cref{def:product}. (ii)$\Rightarrow$(iii): the
$b$-fold realization has $\beta=b$, and $2\leq b\leq n/2<n$ by
\Cref{thm:product}. (iii)$\Rightarrow$(ii): a balanced protocol with $\beta=b$,
$1<b<n$, has arm length $d=n/b$ by \Cref{lem:divisor}, so $n=db$ with both
factors at least $2$, and $\Rc_n=\Rc_d\cprod\Rc_b$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[The sieve of Eratosthenes as removal of the product image]
\label{cor:sieve}
Fix $N\geq 2$ and let
\[
\mathcal{P}_N \;=\; \bigl\{\, \Rc_a\cprod\Rc_b \;:\; a,b\geq 2,\ ab\leq N \,\bigr\}
\;=\; \{\, \Rc_n : n\leq N,\ n\ \text{composite}\,\}.
\]
Then the prime cycles up to $N$ are exactly the complement,
\[
\{\, \Rc_p : p\leq N,\ p\ \text{prime}\,\}
\;=\; \{\,\Rc_2,\dots,\Rc_N\,\}\mathbin{\backslash} \mathcal{P}_N .
\]
That is, forming every product of two nontrivial cycles up to bound $N$ and
striking the periods that occur leaves precisely the primes. The struck set is a
composition in the grammar, not an external divisibility test.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
By \Cref{thm:composite} the periods occurring in $\mathcal{P}_N$ are exactly the
composites $\leq N$, so their complement in $\{2,\dots,N\}$ is the set of primes
$\leq N$. This is the classical sieve \cite{Eratosthenes}: the outer loop ranges
over the factor cycles $\Rc_a$, and the inner product $\Rc_a\cprod\Rc_b$ marks the
multiples $ab$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Primes are the $\cprod$-irreducibles; unique factorization
transfers]
\label{cor:fta}
A cycle $\Rc_n$ with $n\geq 2$ is $\cprod$-irreducible, meaning not equal to
$\Rc_a\cprod\Rc_b$ for any $a,b\geq 2$, if and only if $n$ is prime. Consequently
$(\{\Rc_n\}_{n\geq 1},\cprod,\Rc_1)$ is a commutative monoid whose atoms are the
prime cycles, and the unique factorization of $\mathbb{N}$ into primes transfers
verbatim: each $\Rc_n$ has a factorization into prime cycles that is unique up to
order.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Irreducibility is the negation of \Cref{thm:composite}(ii), which is primality.
The monoid structure is \Cref{thm:product}; the isomorphism $\Rc_n\mapsto n$ onto
$(\mathbb{N}_{\geq 1},\times,1)$ carries atoms to primes and transports the
fundamental theorem of arithmetic \cite{Hardy2008}.
\end{proof}

So primality need not be posed as a property of $n$ alone. It is a fact about the
product: $n$ is prime exactly when the product operation, run over all smaller
nontrivial cycles, never lands on $\Rc_n$. The composites define themselves by
composing, and the primes are what composition leaves untouched.

\section{The same ring: the diagonal SIC stratum}
\label{sec:sic}
%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The period cycle is not only the carrier of arithmetic; it is the carrier of a
quantum-information condition on the same ring $\mathbb{Z}/d\mathbb{Z}$. A
companion program \cite{Chrysopoeia2048} studies the symmetric informationally
complete measurements (SIC-POVMs) in dimension $d$, sets of $d^2$ equiangular
lines realized as the Weyl-Heisenberg orbit of a single fiducial $\psi$ under the
displacement operators $D_{a,b}$. The clock operator $Z$ in that orbit is exactly
the shift of the period-$d$ cycle: $Z$ acts on $\mathbb{C}^d$ by $Z\,e_k=\omega^k
e_k$ with $\omega=e^{2\pi i/d}$, dual to the cyclic shift $X$ whose graph is
$\Rc_d$. The moduli vector $N_k=|\psi_k|^2$ is a nonnegative function on the node
set of $\Rc_d$.

The diagonal ($a=0$) stratum of the SIC condition is, in that companion, a
statement purely about this cyclic vector: the overlaps
$\nu_{0,b}=\langle\psi,Z^b\psi\rangle=\sum_k N_k\,\omega^{bk}$ must satisfy
$|\nu_{0,b}|^2=1/(d+1)$ for all $b\neq 0$, which is proved equivalent to a flat
cyclic autocorrelation,
\[
C_0=\sum_k N_k^2=\frac{2}{d+1},\qquad
C_m=\sum_k N_k N_{k+m}=\frac{1}{d+1}\ \ (m\neq 0).
\]
By the Wiener-Khinchin identity $|\nu_{0,b}|^2=\sum_m C_m\,\omega^{bm}$, a flat
autocorrelation is a flat power spectrum: the sequence $(|\nu_{0,b}|^2)_{b\neq 0}$
is constant, with no privileged mode.

This is the pure-cycle signature. A period cycle carried no fork and no fuse
(\Cref{rem:barecycle}); measured spectrally it is the unbranched macrocycle, and
its spectral response is flat with a degenerate top, exactly the constant, no
privileged mode state, whereas any branching or defect on the cycle raises a
mode above the flat band and breaks the flatness. The diagonal SIC stratum on
$\mathbb{Z}/d\mathbb{Z}$ and the pure-cycle flatness on $\Rc_d$ are the one
condition: the stratum is the requirement that the moduli sit on the cycle as a
flat-spectrum, unbranched distribution. What the companion states as a
number-theoretic condition on the moduli, the cycle exhibits as the flatness of
its own spectrum.

\begin{remark}[The dimension $d=2048$]
\label{rem:d2048}
The target dimension factors in the cycle product as $2048=2^{11}$, so
$\Rc_{2048}=\Rc_2^{\cprod 11}$. The companion's reduction of the $2048$ moduli to
$1024$ Galois-conjugate pairs, $N_{k+d/2}=\sigma(N_k)$, is the order-two
half-period automorphism $k\mapsto k+1024$ of the ring, the same involution of
$\mathbb{Z}/d\mathbb{Z}$ that the product structure acts through. The arithmetic
of the modulus and the equiangularity condition on it are read off one object.
\end{remark}

\section{The ascent}
\label{sec:ascent}
%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\Cref{fig:ascent} draws the construction as an ascent in which each level
recovers information the level below has dropped. At the foot is the flat
twelve-symbol imscription, a projection that aliases distinct integers. Closing
that word into a period cycle recovers the magnitude $V=n$. The cycle product
then multiplies periods, and the composites are its image: a composite period is
a product of two nontrivial cycles, exhibited as $\beta=n/d$ bubbles. The apex is
the sieve residue: the cycles that are not products, which are exactly the
primes. The parity index of the companion note branches off the same cycle as a
second reading of the one object.

\section{Computation}
\label{sec:computation}
%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The construction was checked directly in the grammar's reference implementation.
Each product and closure below is an output of the checker.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Products.} $\Rc_2\cprod\Rc_3=\Rc_6$ closes in its $3$-fold
  realization with $\beta=3$ and in its $2$-fold realization with $\beta=2$;
  $\Rc_{12}$ closes with $\beta=2,3,4$, one per proper cofactor, from the tilings
  of arm length $6,4,3$. Every value of $\beta$ is the bubble count the tiling
  used, as \Cref{thm:product} requires.
\item \textbf{Irreducibles.} The prime cycle $\Rc_7$ admits no product
  realization: its only balanced closing protocol is the single irreducible
  bubble, $\beta=1$. The same holds for every prime from $2$ to $103$, whose
  period cycle reports node count $V=n$, spectral radius $\rho=2$ with zero gap
  (an unbranched macrocycle), and the single-bubble closure.
\item \textbf{The sieve.} Forming the products $\Rc_a\cprod\Rc_b$ for
  $2\leq a\leq b$ up to a bound and striking their periods reproduces the
  composites, and the twenty-seven periods from $2$ to $103$ that remain are
  exactly the primes.
\end{itemize}

The mathematical content is \Cref{thm:product,lem:divisor}, both elementary, and
their formalization in the style of the companion note \cite{OddRingIndex} would
state that the $b$-fold realization of $\Rc_a\cprod\Rc_b$ closes with $\beta=b$
and that a balanced tiling of arm length $d$ exists iff $d\mid n$. From those two
the sieve corollary is a line. That formalization is the natural next artifact;
the checker grounds the statement now, and the kernel should sign it.

\paragraph{Close condition.} A protocol closes when $\fuse\circ\fork$ acts as the
identity on a transformed object: a $\fork$ splits, at least one arm does work,
and a $\fuse$ restores identity. A bare cycle is not a closure, and the circuit
rank alone is not diagnostic.

\paragraph{Ancestry pairing.} A pair $(\fork,\fuse)$ exists when the two in-arms
of the $\fuse$ trace back, along directed edges, to a common $\fork$, however the
arms are routed. Pairing is a property of the edges, not of textual position, so
a word wired two ways pairs differently. The reconvergence multiplicity $\beta$
of \Cref{def:balanced} counts these pairs, and in the $b$-fold realization of a
product it equals the factor $b$ (\Cref{thm:product}). A fuse may have several
qualifying forks; it pairs with the innermost, the one no other candidate
descends from, so a fork may close more than one fuse but a fuse closes with
exactly one fork. A program is \emph{fully closed} only when every fork and every
fuse participates in a pair; one dangler leaves it open.

\paragraph{Verdicts.} The type-checker returns a Belnap value with a reading.

Verdict & Reading & Meaning \\
\midrule
\texttt{T} & closes        & every pair reconnects and at least one carries work \\
\texttt{N} & identity      & forks and fuses reconnect but no work runs between \\
\texttt{N} & no fork/void  & no fork-fuse dyad, or nothing parsed \\
\texttt{B} & open          & well-typed but a fork or fuse dangles \\
\texttt{B} & paradox held  & closes over work \emph{and} an \texttt{⊞} is present \\
\texttt{F} & ill-typed     & a non-fork fans out, a non-fuse merges, or an arity is exceeded \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent A word that closes but contains \texttt{⊞} reports \texttt{B}, never
\texttt{T}: holding a paradox is sound but is not a clean pass. Open valences, an
arm that runs out of successors, are living ends, reported and not fatal.

%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{The trilattice extension (\textsc{sixteen\_3})}
\label{app:sixteen3}
%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

A sibling grammar, \textsc{imasm}-16\_3, extends the alphabet to fourteen
opcodes by adding an arity-three fork and fuse alongside the classic pair, and
replacing the two Boolean evaluators by the four constructive ones. It is the
grammar of the trilattice \textsc{sixteen\_3} \cite{ShramkoDunnTakenaka2001}, the
full powerset of the four-value base $\{T,F,t,f\}$ (constructively proven,
constructively refuted, acceptable, rejectable), sixteen states with $N=\{\}$ at
the bottom and $A=\{T,F,t,f\}$ at the top. It is the register carried by the
\textsc{sixteen\_3} lattice on which the higher-dimensional constructions of the
program are built, and we record it for completeness. The classic twelve-opcode
tower is untouched and sits inside it.

\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.15}
\begin{tabular}{@{}c l l c c@{}}
\toprule
Code & Name & Meaning & Valence & Work \\
\midrule
\texttt{⊢} & VINIT   & source boundary                      & $0\to1$ & no  \\
\texttt{⊣} & TANCH   & sink boundary                        & $1\to1$ & no  \\
\texttt{>} & AFWD    & forward morphism                     & $1\to1$ & yes \\
\texttt{<} & AREV    & reverse morphism                     & $1\to1$ & yes \\
\texttt{=} & CLINK   & compose, link                        & $1\to1$ & yes \\
\texttt{⊙} & IMSCRIB & identity, self-reference             & $1\to1$ & no  \\
\texttt{∈} & FSPLIT3 & three-way split $\fork_3$ (T/F/I arms) & $1\to3$ & no  \\
\texttt{∋} & FFUSE3  & three-way fuse $\fuse_3$             & $3\to1$ & no  \\
\texttt{+} & EVALT   & set $T$ (constructively proven)      & $1\to1$ & yes \\
\texttt{×} & EVALF   & set $F$ (constructively refuted)     & $1\to1$ & yes \\
\texttt{⊞} & EVALI   & set $t$ and $f$ (the information layer) & $1\to1$ & yes \\
\texttt{\textasciitilde} & TNEG & negation, swap $T\leftrightarrow F$ & $1\to1$ & yes \\
\texttt{≁} & INEG    & con-negation, swap $t\leftrightarrow f$ & $1\to1$ & yes \\
\texttt{¬} & IFIX    & irreversible commit                  & $1\to1$ & yes \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The glyph \texttt{⊞} names \textsc{engagr} in the twelve-opcode grammar and
\textsc{evali} here; the reading is fixed by which grammar is in force. The
negations are bit-swaps, not flips, because trilattice negation must preserve the
information order exactly, and a swap preserves the number of set bits. The
register carries three orderings, each with its own meet and join:
\[
\leq_i\ \text{(information)},\qquad
\leq_t\ \text{(truth)},\qquad
\leq_c\ \text{(constructivity)},
\]
and the tri-ancestral close condition is the arity-three form of the pairing
rule: a $(\fork_3,\fuse_3)$ pair exists when all three distinct in-arms of the
$\fuse_3$ trace back to a common $\fork_3$, with the same $T/N/B/F$ readings as
above.
