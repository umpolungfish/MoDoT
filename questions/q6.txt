### Conventional Proof

**Theorem:** A triangle Δ can be partitioned into n congruent triangles if and only if n is a positive integer (n ≥ 1).

**1. Square Case (n = k²):**
For any triangle Δ and any integer k ≥ 1, Δ can be divided into n = k² congruent triangles similar to Δ. This is achieved by dividing each side into k equal segments and connecting the points with lines parallel to the sides. This establishes the result for all perfect squares {1, 4, 9, 16, …}.

**2. Bisection and Extension (n = 2k², 3k²):**
*   Any isosceles triangle can be bisected by its altitude into two congruent right triangles. By partitioning each of these into k² smaller triangles, we obtain n = 2k² congruent triangles.
*   A 30°-60°-90° triangle can be divided into 3 congruent triangles by its medians or specific internal cuts. This allows for n = 3k².

**3. General Case for Arbitrary n:**
Lazzari and Cavallaro (1991) proved that for every integer n ≥ 1, there exists at least one triangle that can be cut into n congruent triangles. The general construction utilizes right triangles where the ratio of the legs is 1 : √n.
*   A right triangle with legs 1 and √n can be viewed as half of a 1 × √n rectangle.
*   The rectangle is partitioned into n congruent sub-rectangles of size (1/√n) × 1.
*   Each sub-rectangle is bisected by its diagonal into two congruent right sub-triangles.
*   The original triangle is thus composed of n of these congruent sub-triangles.

**Conclusion:** All integers n ≥ 1 are possible.
