Let G = (V, E) be the unit distance graph. We demonstrate that no two edges of length 1 can cross. Suppose edges ab and cd intersect at an interior point p. By the triangle inequality:
‖a - c‖ + ‖b - d‖ > (‖a - p‖ + ‖p - c‖) + (‖b - p‖ + ‖p - d‖) = ‖a - b‖ + ‖c - d‖ = 1 + 1 = 2.
If ‖a - c‖ + ‖b - d‖ > 2, then at least one distance (‖a - c‖ or ‖b - d‖) must exceed 1. This contradicts the constraint diam(A) = 1. Thus, G is a geometric graph with no crossing edges.

- **Base Case (n=3):** The maximum unit distances is 3 (equilateral triangle), so |E| ≤ 3.
- **Inductive Step:** If there exists a vertex v with degree d(v) ≤ 1, removing v leaves n-1 points and at most n-1 edges (by hypothesis). Re-adding v adds at most 1 edge, thus |E| ≤ n.
- **Cycle Case:** If every vertex has d(v) ≥ 2, the graph consists of cycles. In a diameter-1 set, even cycles (e.g., C_4) are forbidden because they contain a diagonal > 1. A planar graph of n vertices composed of non-crossing odd cycles satisfies |E| = n.

**Conclusion:** The number of unit distances is at most n.
